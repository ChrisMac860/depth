DEPTH · shift functions on a famous experiment

When the mean isn't as inclusive as it seems.

Chris Mackle · July 2026 · all numbers reproducible from public data (sources in the footer)

In the mid-1970s the United States ran a randomised experiment on a job-training programme; it went by the name of the National Supported Work Demonstration. Take the sample that made it famous (185 people through the programme, 260 controls), compare average earnings in 1978, and you get the result that launched a thousand citations: participants earned $1,794 more on average, with a p-value of 0.008. The programme worked. Approve it and ship it.

Now look at the same 445 people without averaging.

The full self-contained report this figure comes from is here; it opens offline and carries its own provenance.

  • At the 10th percentile, the difference is $0. The people at the bottom of the distribution gained absolutely nothing whatsoever.
  • At the 25th percentile they gained $485.
  • At the median: $1,094.
  • At the 75th: $2,355. At the 90th: $3,198. At the 95th: roughly $3,900.
  • The share of people earning nothing at all fell from 35% to 24%.

The average of $1,794 is true, but it is misleading and does not give the full picture. The chart above is a shift function: at every quantile of the control distribution, it shows how much higher or lower the treatment distribution sits, with a confidence band around the whole curve. It comes from a 1976 paper by Doksum and Sievers ("Plotting with Confidence: Graphical Comparisons of Two Populations") that experimentation tooling simply never picked up. Every A/B dashboard I have seen just reports the difference in means and stops; perhaps, if you are lucky, the 95th percentile.

The detail that keeps it honest rather than a p-hacking machine is the word simultaneous. The band around the curve is valid across the whole curve at once: one confidence statement for every quantile together, not ninety-nine separate tests waiting to hand you a false positive. If you have ever been tempted to peek at "the effect in the top decile" after a flat result, this is the adult version of that instinct.

Applied here, the simultaneous band is also where the humility comes in. With 445 people, only one part of the curve clears the bar outright: around the 38th percentile, where the gap is $1,510. The large gains at the top are real point estimates with wide uncertainty; the honest reading is "suggestive, not proven". A per-quantile dashboard would have handed you all of those numbers with no such warning.

The twist: the famous number is fragile

The $1,794 comes from the Dehejia and Wahba experimental subsample, the version of the data most papers use. The original full experimental sample (297 treated, 425 controls) tells a subtler, quieter story: the mean difference is $886 with p = 0.07, and the shift function finds no significant region anywhere. Same programme, same outcome variable, different sample: the single-number verdict flips from "worked" to "inconclusive". Distributions are not a luxury; they are the context that stops one number from lying to you, adding depth to your choices.

Where this bites in product work

Means hide tails, and tails are where the damage lives and slowly compounds: the slow checkout experiences, the sellers whose earnings fall while the average rises, the users a change quietly breaks. Any metric that is skewed (revenue, latency, delivery time, session length, payout) can move at the top while the bottom goes backwards, and a difference in means will happily net the two against each other. Guardrail and fairness questions are quantile questions.

Scope

Honestly: this method is for continuous metrics with a few hundred observations or more per group. It is not for binary conversion rates; a shift function on zeros and ones tells you nothing a proportion test does not.
